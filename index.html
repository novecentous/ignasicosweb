Research

I am a Research Assistant at the ISIR, Université Pierre et Marie Curie since April 2012.

My main research interest is on the motor control aspects of decision-making. Specifically, I am devoted to elucidate the brain mechanisms underlying decisions between motor actions, and the principles underlying the selection of movement parameters as a function of sensory information, of the structure the motor apparatus and of volition.

In neuroscience, one of the most relevant questions is how the brain prepares actions implying different costs and yielding different payoffs, each associated to a different option, to finally decide a movement of specific kinematic and dynamic properties. Understanding how this operates has both an academic and a clinical interest, to discover the principles underlying the generation of specific movements, and to provide a more comprehensive understanding of volitional disorders such as Parkinson's Disease.

My approach consists of a combination of experimental techniques (Psychophysics and Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation) to investigate the manner in which movement is generated under specific experimental conditions and to probe the operation of specific brain areas, and of computational techniques (Theoretical Models at different levels of description) to investigate the dynamics of the cortex and the basal ganglia during the process of defining the parameters to elicit specific movements. I am actively collaborating with the team of neurophysiologists of David Robbe at the INMED of Marseille, with Paul Cisek at the University of Montreal and with Julie Duqué at the Universite Catholique de Louvain.

I am currently funded by a grant of the Ville de Paris, and I work with Benoit Girard, Emmanuel Guigon and Mehdi Khamassi.
